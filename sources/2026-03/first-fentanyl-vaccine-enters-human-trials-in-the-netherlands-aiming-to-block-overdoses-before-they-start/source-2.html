Scientists Are Testing a Fentanyl Vaccine. Here’s How It Would Work

An experimental shot aims to prevent overdose by trapping fentanyl in the bloodstream.

byTudor Tarita
January 5, 2026
in Health, News
A A
Edited and reviewed by Tibi Puiu

Fentanyl does not need much room to kill. A dose the size of a few grains of salt can slow breathing until it stops. The drug is up to 100 times more potent than morphine. Mixed invisibly into pills and powders by street dealers, it often reaches people who never intended to take the opioid at all.

In the United States, synthetic opioids, primarily fentanyl, are now involved in nearly 70% of overdose deaths, and overdose-reversal drugs hang on public walls in some cities like fire extinguishers. One such antidotes, called Naloxone, has saved many lives, helping drive a decline in overdose deaths in 2024. But it works only after an overdose has begun.

A New York biotech company wants to intervene earlier. Instead of reversing fentanyl’s effects in an emergency, it is developing a vaccine designed to stop the drug before it reaches the brain.

Intercepting the Overdose

Early in 2026, ARMR Sciences plans to launch its first human trial of a fentanyl vaccine. About 40 healthy adults will be enrolled at the Centre for Human Drug Research in Leiden, the Netherlands.

“It became very apparent to me that as I assessed the treatment landscape, everything that exists is reactionary,” said Collin Gage, ARMR’s cofounder and chief executive, in an interview with Wired. “I thought, why are we not preventing this?”

The idea is simple in concept. Naloxone works by pushing opioids off receptors in the brain once breathing has already slowed. A vaccine would work earlier, circulating in the blood and intercepting fentanyl before it can reach those receptors at all.

Opioid vaccines are not new. Researchers explored them in the 1970s, but early heroin vaccines failed to show consistent benefits. Interest faded until the modern overdose crisis—and new immunology tools—pulled the idea back into view, with support from federal agencies and universities.

Fentanyl Antibodies

Vaccines usually target viruses or bacteria, not chemicals. Fentanyl is so small that the immune system barely notices it. On its own, it does not trigger antibody production.

To solve that problem, ARMR’s vaccine links a fentanyl-like fragment to a larger carrier protein called CRM197, a deactivated diphtheria toxin already used in approved vaccines. An added adjuvant, derived from E. coli toxins, boosts the immune response.

Together, these components train the immune system to make antibodies that recognize fentanyl itself. If fentanyl enters the bloodstream of a vaccinated person, antibodies bind to the drug almost immediately.

Normally, fentanyl slips easily through the blood–brain barrier. Once bound to antibodies, it becomes too large to pass. The drug cannot produce a high or suppress breathing and is eventually cleared from the body.

In rat trials led by researchers at the University of Houston with collaborators at Tulane University, the vaccine blocked 92 to 98% of fentanyl from entering the brain. The protection lasted around 20 weeks in rats, raising hopes for long-lasting effects in humans.

Testing the Idea

The upcoming trial will unfold in stages. First, researchers will focus on safety, monitoring volunteers for side effects and measuring antibody levels after a series of injections. Later, a small group of participants will receive very low, clinically standard doses of fentanyl under intensive medical supervision, allowing scientists to see how well the vaccine blocks its effects.

Gage has likened the vaccine to a suit of armor. “This is something that could completely change the paradigm of how we deal with overdose,” he said, “because it doesn’t require someone to be carrying the treatment on them.”

Still, limits are clear. Antibodies are finite, and in theory, a large enough dose of fentanyl could overwhelm them. “There’s only going to be so many antibodies,” said Sharon Levy, an addiction specialist at Boston Children’s Hospital and an adviser to ARMR, also speaking to Wired.

There are also questions about pain care. Fentanyl remains important in anesthesia and emergency medicine. Researchers say animal studies show the vaccine does not interfere with other opioids such as morphine, methadone, or buprenorphine, leaving alternatives available.

Targeted Protection

Surveys conducted by addiction researchers suggest interest in a fentanyl vaccine among teenagers and young adults at risk of accidental exposure, as well as among people in treatment for opioid use disorder. “Overall, our experience has been that people would be interested in this,” Levy said.

Other scientists are pursuing related strategies, including monoclonal antibody drugs that provide about a month of protection. “We think a month of protection is pretty good in terms of providing a safety net,” said Marco Pravetoni, whose team is developing such an approach, as per Wired.

No one involved claims a vaccine would end addiction or dismantle the illicit drug market. At best, it would add another layer of protection in a crisis that has touched nearly half of American families.

Gage often returns to that personal toll. “I had two close childhood friends who passed away from fentanyl overdose,” he told Live Science. “Neither of them were seeking it out.”

Whether ARMR’s vaccine fulfills its promise will depend on careful clinical testing. If it succeeds, overdose prevention may begin not with an emergency response, but with immunity built long before fentanyl has a chance to do harm.
