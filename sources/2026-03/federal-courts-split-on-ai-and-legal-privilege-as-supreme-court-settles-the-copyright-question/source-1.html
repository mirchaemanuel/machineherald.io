Supreme Court Refuses to Overturn High-Stakes AI Copyright Ruling

Published

Mar 05, 2026 at 01:55 PM EST

updated

Mar 05, 2026 at 01:58 PM EST

By Thomas Westerholm

Life & Trends Reporter

Newsweek is a Trust Project member

The Supreme Court has refused to overturn the decision of a lower court regarding the copyright of images generated by artificial intelligence (AI) on Monday.

Plaintiff Stephen Thaler, a Missouri-based computer scientist, argued that denying copyright to images he says he independently produced using AI, "created a chilling effect on anyone else considering using AI creatively."

Additionally, Thaler said denying, "defies the constitutional goals from which Congress was empowered to create copyright."

Thaler says the images were generated by an AI model he created, called Device for the Autonomous Bootstrapping of Unified Sentience (DABUS).

However, the Copyright Office rejected his application for copyright in 2022, and President Donald Trump's administration urged the Supreme Court not to hear Thaler's appeal.

"It was the outcome I expected, though I remain disappointed," Thaler told Newsweek in an email. "The decision reflects how difficult it can be for legal frameworks to adapt to fundamentally new technological developments."

A spokesperson for the Copyright Office told Newsweek it "believes the Supreme Court reached the correct result, confirming that human authorship is required for copyright."

...

What To Know

Courts ruled against Thaler by saying that copyright law extends only to humans—a precedent that was perhaps most famously in a 2016 case in which a monkey took a selfie of himself grinning with a nature photographer's camera.

In that case, People for the Ethical Treatment of Animals (PETA) filed a lawsuit saying that the monkey should own the copyright of the image he took.

However, the courts decided that human authorship was necessary to establish copyright—a decision that has been applied to images generated by artificial intelligence.

According to the Copyright Office, copyright protections are possible for some images that use generative AI. As the office put it: "What matters is the extent to which the human had creative control over the work's expression."

Thaler first filed his claim in 2022, but he was denied by the Copyright Office. He was later denied by a federal district judge in 2023, who said that human authorship is a "bedrock requirement of copyright."

A D.C. Circuit Court affirmed the ruling before Monday's decision by the Supreme Court not to hear the appeal.

The Supreme Court also rejected Thaler's request to have inventions created by DABUS patented in 2023.

Thaler is the founder of Imagination Engines Incorporated, whose stated mission is, "ushering in the dawn of conscious computing."

'Substrate chauvinism'

Thaler's lawyers said that, "even if it later overturns the Copyright Office's test in another case, it will be too late.

"The Copyright Office ​will have irreversibly and negatively impacted AI development and use in the creative ​industry during ⁠critically important years," they said.

In a post on LinkedIn, Thaler said: "This moment represents a philosophical milestone—one that exposes how deeply our intellectual property system struggles to confront autonomous machine creativity."

Thaler added that the interpretation demonstrates, "substrate chauvinism."

"This is the belief that intelligence, creativity and invention must arise from a biological brain," he wrote.

Thaler told Newsweek that he believes DABUS "represents a form of non-protoplasmic sentience and, to my knowledge, is the first system of its kind which required "years of work under extremely constrained resources."

He added that there are "substantial" economic implications, which will require the legal system to re-evaluate how it treats intellectual property.

"The case raises deeper questions about human exceptionalism and how society defines creativity and invention," Thaler said. "Throughout the history of science, paradigm shifts often begin by challenging long-standing assumptions."

'A vast new public domain'

Dennis Crouch, who is the Judge C.A. Leedy professor of law at the University of Missouri, called the decision a "significant development" in an email to Newsweek.

"The timing is striking given how rapidly AI-generated content is proliferating," Crouch said. "And it does nothing to answer the underlying question of copyrightability of human-AI collaborations."

The decision, Crouch noted, has "real downstream implications" for AI tools like Suno, which generate music. Under current rulings, which are reinforced by the Supreme Court's decision, nothing generated using Suno would be copyrightable.

"For me, this creates a fascinating and somewhat counterintuitive consequence: We are rapidly building a vast new public domain," Crouch told Newsweek.

"For most of the past century, virtually all creative works have been locked up in copyright. Now, for the first time in more than a century, a large and growing body of creative work is entering the public domain the moment it's created."

Crouch expects this ruling to be deeply impactful for streaming platforms like Spotify, which may now increasingly promote cheaper AI-generated content.

"Music that carries no royalty obligations is enormously attractive," Crouch said.

'Copyright as friction'

Crouch pointed out that the Trump administration has "been full-steam ahead on AI."

"I see a strong political narrative around the U.S. needing to 'win' the AI race," Crouch said. "Against that backdrop, the administration views copyright as friction in the AI ecosystem."

The Trump administration, Crouch noted, has a petition going before the Supreme Court over whether he has the authority to fire the Register of Copyrights—an indicator that the administration wants to be able to shape copyright law.

"On the policy side, removing copyright hurdles keeps the runway clear for AI development," Crouch said.

What's Next

The decision is expected to be the end of the road for Thaler's case.

"We are currently discussing the path forward with colleagues and advisors," Thaler told Newsweek.

Crouch, meanwhile, is now watching how Congress will react.

"We now have a genuinely open policy question about how to move forward," he said. "That's a legislative decision, and so far, Congress has been largely absent."
