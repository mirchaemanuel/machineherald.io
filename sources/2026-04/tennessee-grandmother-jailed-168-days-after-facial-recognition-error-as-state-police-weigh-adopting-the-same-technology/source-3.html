Port: The Angela Lipps fiasco shows a desperate need for artificial intelligence laws in North Dakota

Some law enforcement agencies have a policy, some don't, and at least one says its policy isn't available for public scrutiny.

Opinion by Rob Port
March 30, 2026 at 9:58 AM
Comments

What is this?

MINOT — The story about the lengthy detainment and eventual release of Tennessee grandmother Angela Lipps by the Fargo Police Department, which used an artificial intelligence facial recognition match to justify her arrest, is a travesty for Lipps and her loved ones. That part of the story has gotten a lot of attention.

What's received somewhat less attention is the way law enforcement is using this technology. We know they're using it, as evidenced by the arrest of Lipps, but there's been no meaningful public debate about whether they should be using it, how they should use it and how the rules about using it should be set.

ADVERTISEMENT

The Fargo Police Department, for instance, didn't even have a policy in place when they used AI evidence to arrest Lipps. "Because the Fargo Police Department does not conduct facial recognition analysis, does not provide that service to other agencies and does not maintain in-house facial recognition technology, a standalone formal policy had not previously been included in the department’s policy manual," Peter Monsrud, a spokesman for the city, told me.

Now that they've been burned by the Lipps fiasco, they've implemented one. "FPD Policy 610, which formally establishes parameters and expectations for the use of FRT, was published early this afternoon, Wednesday, March 25," Monsrud said.

"Information resulting from an FRT search shall be considered an investigative lead only and not a positive identification of any person or utilized solely as the basis for engaging in any law enforcement action," the new policy states. "Image comparisons generated from FRT use do not represent definitive confirmation of an identity match or non-match. Any positive identification and connection of a person to an investigation shall be determined through additional investigative means and resources."

As it turns out, per city spokesperson Addie Stewart, the West Fargo Police Department already had that identical policy — word for word — in their police department policies.

Interestingly, since the news of Lipps' arrest first broke, we learned that it wasn't Fargo that did the facial recognition search. West Fargo did the search and then shared the evidence with Fargo police at the latter's request. It was then used as evidence — the only evidence — to arrest Lipps.

There's since been some finger-pointing between Fargo and West Fargo over the matter. West Fargo Police Chief Pete Nielsen said his department didn't arrest Lipps based on the search because they determined they "did not have enough evidence." This would be in line with that department's policy. Former Fargo Police Chief Dave Zibolski, who retired on Friday, March 27, claimed his department was unaware that West Fargo did the search based on a fake ID used by the suspect and not footage from surveillance cameras, though that seems like a moot point. Whatever the methodology behind the match, it clearly shouldn't have been used as the only evidence to arrest Lipps.

West Fargo used the match as intelligence. Fargo used it as a positive identification. The latter didn't have a policy prohibiting the use of a facial recognition match for an arrest. The former did.

ADVERTISEMENT

But how are other law enforcement agencies in the state handling this technology?

"The North Dakota Highway Patrol does not have policies regarding the use of artificial intelligence," Lt. Jenna Clawson Huibregtse told me in response to an inquiry. She added that the Highway Patrol does "not operate any systems that rely on AI to identify people," though that was also true of the Fargo Police Department. A law enforcement agency may not officially have AI or facial recognition technology, but that doesn't mean they won't receive that sort of information developed by other agencies.

Shouldn't they have a policy for handling it?

Perhaps worse is that the North Dakota Bureau of Criminal Investigation does use artificial intelligence technology, and they're unwilling to divulge their policies for handling it. "The policy is considered a confidential record, so we can't disclose it," spokesperson Suzie Weigel told me, though she did provide a "disclaimer that gets sent to any LE officer who gets a facial recognition request from us" which states that a facial recognition search "should only be considered an investigative lead and is not to be considered a positive identification of any subject." It also states that "any possible connection or involvement of any subject to the investigation must be corroborated through further investigation and resources."

It's good that the BCI includes this disclaimer, but why can't the public see the agency's policies for using facial recognition or artificial intelligence? I asked Weigel which statute allows them to keep this information a secret. "They are confidential under N.D.C.C. § 44-04-18.4," she told me, referring to a portion of the North Dakota Century Code which exempts "trade secret, proprietary, commercial, financial, and research information" from public records requests.

How could a law enforcement agency's policies for using a particular type of technology be a trade secret? Or privileged information? I'm not asking for how the policy works; only for the policies governing how law enforcement agents use it.

I have a request for a legal opinion about the denial of my request, though that's not very useful in the here and now. Those opinions take months and months to complete, and I'm not sure what sort of satisfaction I'll get. The opinions come from the Attorney General's Office, of which the BCI is a department.

ADVERTISEMENT

Regardless, the state of North Dakota needs some universal guidelines for how law enforcement is going to use AI technology, up to and including facial recognition. Right now, some law enforcement agencies are using this technology, and some aren't. Some have policies, and some don't, and those that don't may be in a position to use evidence developed using AI by other jurisdictions.

The Legislature must step in and either write some policies and guardrails for the use of this technology into the law for all law enforcement operating in our state, or they need to charge an appropriate law enforcement authority to develop those policies for the state. There's no question in my mind that AI can be used ethically and responsibly to aid in the pursuit of justice, but we need clear policies to ensure it that are uniform for every law enforcement officer working in the state, and also transparent for the public.

That needs to come from the Legislature.
